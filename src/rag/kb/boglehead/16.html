From Bogleheads
For a full discussion, see wikipedia:Market capitalization.

Market capitalization, also referred to as "market cap", is the total value of all outstanding shares of a publicly traded company multiplied by the stock price. It is commonly used to measure the size of a corporation, and represents the market's collective valuation of a company. The investment community uses this figure to classify companies by size, rather than using revenue or total assets.

The stocks of large, medium and small companies are referred to as large caps, mid caps, and small caps, respectively. There's no universal agreement on how these classifications correspond to company values, and the relationship changes over time as the aggregate value of all public companies changes. This table shows a typical mapping, as of 2025.[1]

Table 1. Market capitalization
Size characterization Capitalization range
Large capitalization stocks Over $10 billion dollars
Mid capitalization stocks Between $2 billion and $10 billion dollars
Small capitalization stocks Between $300 million and $2 billion dollars

These ranges will vary across index providers, investment firms, and other market observers.

Less commonly used labels include: mega cap (bigger than large cap), and micro and nano cap (smaller than small cap).

Because shares of private companies do not trade in public markets, they do not have a market capitalization. Their value is estimated based on revenue multiples, comparable company analysis, or investor negotiations.
